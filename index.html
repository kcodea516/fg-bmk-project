Abstract

Recent advancements in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated remarkable multimodal perception capabilities, garnering significant attention. While numerous evaluation studies have emerged, assessing LVLMs both holistically and on specialized tasks, fine-grained image tasks—fundamental to computer vision—remain largely unexplored. To fill this gap, we introduce a comprehensive fine-grained evaluation benchmark, i.e., FG-BMK, comprising 1.01 million questions and 0.28 million images. Our evaluation systematically examines LVLMs from both human-oriented and machine-oriented perspectives, focusing on their semantic recognition and fine-grained feature representation capabilities. Through extensive experiments on twelve representative LVLMs/VLMs, we uncover key findings regarding the influence of training paradigms, modality alignment, perturbation susceptibility, and fine-grained category reasoning on task performance. This work provides critical insights into the limitations of current LVLMs and offers guidance for future data construction and model design in the development of more advanced LVLMs. Our code is open-source and available at https://github.com/SEU-VIPGroup/FG-BMK.

Key Findings

Core insights from extensive experiments on 12 representative LVLMs/VLMs

Training Paradigm is Crucial
Contrastive training paradigms are significantly more effective at enhancing fine-grained visual discriminability compared to generative or reconstruction-based approaches.
Penalty for Granularity Mismatch
Aligning visual and text features can actually impair fine-grained discriminability if there is a severe mismatch in data granularity.
Vulnerability to Perturbations
LVLMs and VLMs are far more sensitive to feature perturbations in fine-grained tasks than in generic vision tasks.
Perception vs. Reasoning
While LVLMs show strong appearance perception, they struggle with reasoning for fine-grained categories that rely on subtle visual attributes.
Advantage of Specialized Models
Current LVLMs still lag behind specialized fine-grained tailored models when handling complex, domain-specific visual recognition.

Why FG-BMK?

FG-BMK introduces Two Complementary Evaluation Perspectives to systematically evaluate both conversational multimodal understanding and direct visual feature representation.

Human-oriented

Task 1: Hierarchical Granularity Recognition

Q: What is the species of the bird in this image?

(A) Great Crested Flycatcher   (B) Eastern Phoebe ...

Testing the model's domain-specific knowledge across different taxonomic levels (e.g., Class → Genus → Species) within the same meta-category.

Aviation Dataset Preview

Human-oriented

Task 2: Knowledge Bias Estimation

Q: Is the aircraft model in this image a Boeing 737-800?

(A) True   (B) False

Uncovering potential blind spots and language-prior biases where models may rely on prior knowledge rather than visual cues for highly similar categories.

Human-oriented

Task 3: Attribute Recognition

Q: Is the petal color of the flower in this image pink?

(A) True   (B) False

Evaluates sensitivity to minute visual details such as color, shape, and texture at the fine-grained level, bypassing coarse category recognition.

Evaluation Framework

A dual paradigm bridging human perception and machine feature representation

Human-oriented

Dialogue-based evaluation of semantic recognition through conversational queries.

Attribute Recognition — sensitivity to color, shape, texture
Knowledge Bias — visual cues vs. language priors
Hierarchical Reasoning — Class → Genus → Species

Machine-oriented

Direct assessment of visual backbone feature representation quality.

Fine-grained Retrieval — mAP across subordinate categories
Fine-grained Classification — Top-1 accuracy on visual features

Bypasses language generation to isolate pure visual perception.
